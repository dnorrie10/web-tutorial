Total Quality Management

Impact of Quality on Cycle Time and Cost

1. Overview

During the manufacture of a product and its component parts, each operation has at least one opportunity to create a defect. Quality is defined in the ISO standard 8402 – 1986, 3.1 as: ‘The totality of features and characteristics of a product or service that bear on its ability to satisfy stated or implied needs.’ The less defects present in the project, the higher the quality. The aim of this section is to demonstrate the relationship of defects to manufacturing cycle time cost.

2. Defects

There are two types of defect that may creep into a product during the manufacturing process:

  1. Obvious defects are easily detected and corrected in-process. Inspection and testing are effective tools used to detect obvious defects.
  2. Latent defects are not readily detected by normally applied test and inspection methods. These will most likely still be in the product that is delivered to the customer

Manufacturing controls the creation of latent defects. The design and execution of the processes used to reproduce the product is the major determinant of the average latent defect per unit content of the product. The capability of the processes used to reproduce the product is the major determinant of the degree of abnormality of latent defects.

Design controls the constant failure rate. The design of the product is the major determinant of the inherent (constant) failure rate, which will be attained by the product after all early failures have been discovered and corrected. Lack of operating margin or marginal overstress tend to appear as constant rate failures.

Only integrated product/process design for manufacturability and subsequent tight process control can eliminate latent defects because this prevents these defects from being created in the first place.

3. Strategy to Improve Quality

The following steps can be taken to improve the quality of a product:

  1. Use the fewest number of parts.
  2. Use parts of known capability.
  3. Use the lowest possible stress levels.
  4. Avoid marginal over-stress.
  5. Provide maximum possible operating margins and mechanical tolerances

4. Effect of defects on Manufacturing Costs

Defects cost money as a result of:

  • Diagnostic time
  • Repair time/Repair/Re-inspection
  • Extra inventory
  • Extra labour
  • Extra materials
  • Extra capacity (equipment, floor space)
  • Extra support
  • Cost of failure * (internal) = dpu x volume x average cost/defect
  • Not included here is the cost of warranty and the replacement of escaping defects.

5. Inventory and Cycle Time

Inventory includes all piece parts, wafers assemblies, kits and models in stockrooms, count stations, in process, and in transit to and from other facilities.Inventory costs include:

  • Cost of raw material storage
  • Finished goods inventory
  • Delivery times

Cycle time is the total clock or calendar time from start to completion of an event. Includes move time, setup/changeover time, wait time,‘first in first out’ queue time, as well as processing time for manufactured product.Inventory and cycle time are related by the following equation:

Cycle time = inventory / throughput rate

6. How Defects Affect Cycle Time

Since we have seen that the distribution of defects follows the Poisson, then it must also be true that we can predict:

  • The ‘test and inspection’ workload to discover defects, and
  • The ‘analyze and repair’ workload to correct defects
  • These two measures determine the cycle time devoted to test and repair, and help predict:
  • The required capacity for test/inspection, analysis, and repair of equipment.
